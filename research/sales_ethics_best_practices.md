# Sales Best Practices & Ethics for Residential Construction — Research Findings

> Research conducted 2026-05-01. Sources cited inline. For CMB's Site Visit App + cmb-war-room cold outreach.

---

## TL;DR — top 10 takeaways

1. **Discovery first, pitch never.** Consultative selling beats transactional in construction by a wide margin — especially at premium price points.

2. **The first conversation is for listening, not selling.** Ask 7-10 discovery questions before discussing your process.

3. **Transparency about pricing, schedule, and risk increases close rate, not decreases it.** Studies consistently show buyers prefer honest builders even when they cost more.

4. **CAN-SPAM penalty: $53,088 per email** (as of Jan 2025). No B2B exemption. Every cold email needs physical address, honest header, working unsubscribe, honored within 10 business days.

5. **Montana 28-2-2201 requires** specific written disclosures in residential construction contracts: warranty (1-yr minimum express), GL/WC insurance status, payment schedules, change-order procedures, and owner rights.

6. **The "we need to think about it" objection is a certainty problem, not a money problem.** Don't lower the price — surface the doubt.

7. **Cash allowances build trust. Bait allowances destroy reputations.** Disclose the supplier invoice price separately from your markup. Clients fact-check.

8. **Three-tier pricing (Core / Plus / Premium) reduces price objections without changing your baseline.** Premium = 1.8-2.2x Core, includes high-perceived-value adds.

9. **Most builders accidentally damage trust early** through over-promising, false urgency, "other guys" comparisons, fear-mongering, and jargon. Avoid all five.

10. **CMB's neighborly voice is correctly aligned with consultative-selling research.** The bigger risk is drift back to corporate / transactional language under deadline pressure.

## The first conversation — discovery framework

### Industry-standard consultative selling
Per [Seismic](https://www.seismic.com/enablement-explainers/consultative-selling/) and [Salesforce](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/consultative-sales-approach/):

> "Consultative selling encourages sellers to build customer relationships, develop trust, and provide expert advice. Rather than pitching a product or service and completing an impersonal transaction, sellers take the time to fully understand their customer's needs."

### Discovery questions specifically for home builders
From [Builder Lead Converter](https://www.builderleadconverter.com/blog/consultative-selling-home-builders/), adapted for design-build:

1. **"Since we'd be the first builder you've worked with [or: the next builder you'd consider], what would you like to know about us before you'd consider hiring us?"** — hands the mic to the buyer
2. "What's the story of how you came to want to build/remodel this property?"
3. "Tell me what you've been imagining when you picture yourself living in this finished space."
4. "What concerns do you have about this process? What worries you most?"
5. "Have you worked with any builders or designers before? What worked and what didn't?"
6. "When you picture being moved in, what does the day-to-day look like?"
7. "Is there a date you're hoping to be in by? What's driving it?"
8. "Who's involved in this decision? Anyone else we should be including in conversations?"
9. "If we go forward, what's your preferred way to stay in the loop — email, calls, walk-throughs?"
10. "What questions do you have for me?"

### The "budget conversation"
- **When to introduce it:** After you've understood what they want, NOT at the start. Industry consensus from [Buildbook](https://buildbook.co/blog/home-builders-profit-margin), [Building Advisor](https://buildingadvisor.com/), and NAHB.
- **How to introduce it:** "To make this real, I need to ground our conversation in numbers. What range have you been thinking about for total project cost? If you don't have a number yet, that's fine — I'll walk you through what projects like yours have actually cost."
- **If they say "I don't have a number":** That's normal at first conversation. Give them a range with caveats: "Based on what you've described, I'd expect this to land between $X and $Y. We'd tighten that range as we go through design."

## Trust building — what works and what destroys trust

### What earns trust with high-end residential clients

Per [Simon-Kucher](https://www.simon-kucher.com/en/insights/consultative-selling-building-trust-and-driving-success) and [Allego](https://www.allego.com/blog/consultative-selling/):

- **Listening more than speaking** — ratio should be 70/30 buyer/you
- **Specific references to their property/project** — proves you paid attention
- **Honest acknowledgement of trade-offs** — "you can have it fast OR cheap OR exceptionally well-built, pick two"
- **Naming the unknowns** — "I don't know what's behind this wall until we open it; let's plan for X possibilities"
- **Following through on small commitments** — "I'll send you that supplier link by tomorrow" → send it that night
- **Showing the math** — when discussing budget, show how the number was built, not just the total

### What destroys trust (the five most common builder errors)

From [Building Advisor](https://buildingadvisor.com/), industry forums, and customer-complaint research:

1. **Over-promising** — "We can definitely have you in by Christmas" when the truth is "we'll know more after design"
2. **False urgency** — "Lumber prices are going up next week, you need to sign now"
3. **"Other guys" comparisons** — disparaging other builders shrinks YOU, not them
4. **Fear-mongering** — "You don't want what happened to my last client to happen to you" (predatory language)
5. **Construction jargon** — using LVL, R-value, P-trap as if everyone knows them; reads as gatekeeping

**CMB's brand voice anchor already prohibits 1, 3, 4, 5.** The risk is drift back into them under deadline pressure. The Site Visit App's AI prompts should hard-block these phrases.

## Premium positioning — handling price

### The "we need to think about it" objection

Per [Service Monster](https://www.servicemonster.com/blog/overcoming-a-customers-price-objections), [Allego](https://www.allego.com/blog/6-common-sales-objections-and-how-to-respond/), and [Sales Insights Lab](https://salesinsightslab.com/handling-price-objections/):

**Real translation:** "I'm not certain yet, and I don't know what I'm uncertain about."

**Wrong response:** lower the price, add freebies, push for commitment.

**Right response:** surface the doubt.
- "Sounds great — what's on your list to think through? Maybe I can help while we're together."
- "Take all the time you need. What questions am I leaving you with?"
- "Often when folks say 'let me think about it,' there's a specific concern. Anything specific I can help with?"

### Three-tier pricing reduces objections

From [Service Monster](https://www.servicemonster.com/blog/overcoming-a-customers-price-objections):

> "Create three options: Core, Plus, Premium. Price Premium at 1.8x to 2.2x Core and include something buyers value that doesn't kill your margin (priority scheduling, quarterly reviews, extra training session). Measure how often Core feels 'reasonable' after showing Premium."

For CMB design-build, this could be:
- **Core** — standard 5-step Design-Build Program at standard pricing
- **Plus** — adds: weekly in-person walkthrough during construction, quarterly post-completion check-ins for year 1
- **Premium** — adds: dedicated PM, 24-month warranty extension, post-completion maintenance plan, complimentary design refresh after 5 years

### "We may not be the cheapest" — the script

Premium builders shouldn't apologize for premium pricing. From [Sales Growth Company](https://salesgrowth.com/overcome-price-objections/):

> "Embrace the fact that you offer premium pricing to increase your profitability. Confidence in your pricing is key; don't engage in price negotiations or try to compete solely on cost."

CMB-voiced version:
> "We're not the cheapest builder in the valley, and we never want to be — we'd rather build twenty homes a year exceptionally well than fifty homes okay. If price is the most important thing, I respect that, and I'd rather you find the right fit. If long-term peace of mind matters more, that's where we shine."

## Written proposals as a sales tool

### What high-converting design-build proposals contain

From [DBIA](https://dbia.org/best-practices/), [Shawn McCadden](https://www.remodelmgmt.com/), and [NAHB](https://www.nahb.org/):

1. **Opening letter from owner** — personal, named, references the conversation
2. **Project understanding** — what we heard you want; this proves listening
3. **Scope of work** — what's included, specific
4. **What's NOT included** — exclusions stated honestly, not buried in fine print
5. **Schedule** — phases, durations, key milestones, Montana seasonal constraints
6. **Investment** — the budget, with how it was built
7. **Process** — the 5-step Design-Build Program, what happens when
8. **What you can expect from us** — the three care behaviors
9. **What we need from you** — selections, decisions, payments
10. **Signature page** — clean, professional, terms readable

### Common proposal mistakes
- ALL CAPS section headers (already on CMB anti-pattern list)
- "We look forward to partnering with you" filler (already banned)
- Dense paragraph blocks unbroken by white space
- Legal-heavy fine print without a friendly summary
- Pricing buried after 12 pages of preamble (lead with it)
- Single dollar number with no range (false precision)
- Range so wide it's useless ($300k–$900k)

### CMB current state
The proposal Word doc already does most of this correctly. Areas to polish:
- Lead the proposal with the personal letter on page 1 (currently might be on page 2)
- Range presentation: state the AACE Class 5 caveat upfront ("conceptual budget, tightens through Steps 1-4")
- "What's not included" section — currently good, keep developing it

## Outreach ethics & compliance

### CAN-SPAM (federal — applies to ALL commercial email)
Per [FTC](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business) and [eCFR 16 CFR Part 316](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-316):

**Required in every commercial email:**
- Header info (From, To, Reply-To, routing) must be accurate
- Subject line cannot be deceptive
- Identify as advertisement (can be implicit if context is clear)
- Physical postal address (PO box OK if registered with USPS)
- Working unsubscribe mechanism
- Honor unsubscribes within **10 business days**
- Don't sell or transfer email addresses of unsubscribers

**Penalties (as of Jan 2025 inflation adjustment):**
- **Up to $53,088 per email in violation** ([Prospeo CAN-SPAM Penalties 2026](https://prospeo.io/s/can-spam-penalties))
- Aggravating factors (deceptive headers, harvested addresses) can double penalties
- FTC's largest CAN-SPAM penalty to date: Verkada paid $2.95M

**No B2B exemption.** Every commercial email — cold outreach to homeowners or to LLCs — must comply.

### Montana 28-2-2201 (state — residential construction contracts)
Per [MCA](https://archive.legmt.gov/bills/mca/title_0280/chapter_0020/part_0220/section_0010/0280-0020-0220-0010.html):

Required disclosures in residential construction contracts:
- General liability insurance disclosure (have policy, or are independent contractor without employees)
- Workers' comp insurance disclosure (same)
- Payment schedule
- Change order procedure
- Statement of inspections, tests, owner rights
- **Express warranty for at least 1 year from completion**, describing components, length, specialty provisions

### Montana contractor registration
Per [MT DLI](https://erd.dli.mt.gov/work-comp-regulations/montana-contractor/construction-contractor-registration):

- Required: any person/business performing construction work with employees
- $70 fee, valid 2 years
- Independent contractors without employees: not required but can choose to
- Verify CMB's registration is current; visible registration number on contracts is standard practice

### CMB current state on compliance
The cmb-war-room outreach drafter (`agents/outreach-drafter/index.js`) already:
- Includes PO Box 2471 mailing address verbatim
- Ends with "Reply STOP to remove from this list"
- Has a code validator that regex-checks for forbidden patterns
- Does not send directly — Matt approves before send

That's CAN-SPAM-compliant for the outreach side. The Site Visit App's contract should be cross-checked against MT 28-2-2201 — current contract appears to cover most provisions but a Montana construction attorney review is recommended.

## Outreach cadence ethics

From email-deliverability and consumer-protection research:

### What's legitimate
- One personalized outreach to a homeowner who pulled a permit
- 3-5 follow-ups over 30 days if no response, each genuinely additive
- Immediate stop on "no," "not interested," or unsubscribe
- No follow-ups to people who explicitly declined

### What crosses into harassment
- Daily emails
- "Pretending" not to have heard a previous "no"
- Re-adding unsubscribers to lists
- Buying lists of recent permits and blasting
- SMS without prior consent (different law — TCPA, with much higher penalties)

### CMB cmb-war-room current cadence
Per the outreach-drafter code: 3, 4, 7, 16 days between touches, then pause. That's well within ethical bounds.

## Sales conversation framework — neighborly approach

### Consultative vs transactional
| Transactional | Consultative |
|---|---|
| Push features | Ask about needs |
| Talk 70% | Listen 70% |
| Close hard | Close soft |
| "Sign today" | "Take your time" |
| Specs and SKUs | Stories and outcomes |
| Same pitch every time | Same questions every time, different conversation |

### CMB voice maps to consultative
The brand voice anchor (`research/voice_anchor_v2.md`) is structurally consultative:
- "Listens before it pitches"
- "Three care behaviors" instead of three service lines
- "Bring clarity to your dreams" instead of "deliver value"
- Three-tiered acknowledgement: "no pitch / take your time / if you have someone you trust that's wonderful too"

### Maintaining the voice under pressure
The biggest risk: at the end of the month, when revenue is short, the temptation is to push harder. The voice drift looks like:
- "I'd love to get started this week"
- "We have a slot opening up in our schedule"
- "Lumber prices are moving"

**None of those are dishonest.** They're just transactional. The neighborly version:
- "We're available when you're ready"
- "Whenever it makes sense for your timeline"
- "Lumber's been volatile — happy to talk about how that affects budget when you're ready to dig in"
