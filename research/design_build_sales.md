# Design-Build Sales & Positioning — Research Findings

> Research conducted 2026-05-01. Sources cited inline. Specific to CMB's 5-step Design-Build Program and competitive positioning vs GCs and architect-led builds in Flathead Valley.

---

## TL;DR — top 10 takeaways

1. **CMB's biggest sales advantage is single-point accountability — but most clients don't know it.** Articulate it early in every conversation.

2. **The 3D walkthrough is the moment-of-truth.** Per CMB facts, this is where clients regularly change major layouts before framing — saving them lifelong regret AND saving CMB from costly change orders. Lead with this.

3. **Industry-standard design retainer for residential design-build is 1-3% of estimated construction cost.** Higher retainers ($20K-30K) are normal for larger custom builds. CMB's current model fits this.

4. **DBIA recommends owner-led selection based on capability + values + chemistry, not low bid.** Position CMB to be evaluated on those criteria.

5. **First right of refusal should have a time window (30-60 days post-Step 4) and price-match mechanism.** CMB's current contract doesn't specify either.

6. **200% design licensing fee is aggressive.** Enforceable in most jurisdictions but rarely tested. Worth a Montana attorney review.

7. **Premium positioning works when you can articulate WHY you cost more.** CMB's three care behaviors do this implicitly; make them explicit in the proposal.

8. **The "I have plans from another architect" objection is the most common — and the most CMB needs to handle gracefully.** Don't refuse those clients; create a clear path.

9. **A salaried owner-led sales model is correct for CMB's revenue tier.** Hand-off to dedicated salesperson typically happens around $5-7M revenue.

10. **Most small design-build firms don't differentiate from GCs in customer language.** That confusion costs deals. CMB should over-correct toward "design-build" terminology in every customer-facing surface.

## Design-build vs GC vs architect-led — the value proposition

### What clients understand (often wrong)
- "GC = builder" — true
- "Architect = designer" — true
- "Design-build = ???" — most clients don't have a mental model

### What clients actually buy
- **Architect-led:** "Get the design I dream of, then put it out to bid." Risks: design exceeds budget, multiple parties to coordinate, blame-shifting when issues arise.
- **GC-led:** "Hire a builder, they'll figure out the design with their preferred architect." Risks: design constrained by builder's preferences, weaker design quality.
- **Design-build:** "One team handles design AND construction, accountable for both." Strengths: schedule compression, single accountability, budget-aware design from day one.

### DBIA findings on design-build outcomes
Per [DBIA Best Practices](https://dbia.org/best-practices/) and the [DBIA Done Right Primer](https://dbianw.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/Design-Build-Best-Practices-Primer-2023.pdf):

Design-build projects compared to design-bid-build (industry-wide, building sector):
- **33% faster schedule** (design + construction overlapped)
- **6% lower unit cost** on average
- **3.6% fewer change orders**
- **Higher owner satisfaction** on schedule and cost predictability

### The "single point of accountability" pitch — how to say it without sounding salesy
> "If something goes wrong on a typical build, the architect blames the builder, the builder blames the architect, and the homeowner is in the middle wondering who to call. With us, there's one phone number — and we answer it whether you're three days into framing or three years into living in it."

That's already in the CMB voice anchor. Just make sure it gets used.

## The 5-step design-build process

### CMB's current 5 steps (per Site Visit App contract)
1. Initial Consultation + Vision Planning
2. Schematic Design
3. Design Development
4. Project Development
5. Construction (separate contract)

### DBIA standard 5 phases (residential adaptation)
1. **Programming** — needs assessment, site analysis, budget framework
2. **Schematic Design** — floor plans, exterior elevations, preliminary specifications
3. **Design Development** — refined plans, structural input, MEP coordination, material selections
4. **Construction Documents** — permit-ready drawings, full specifications, subcontractor bid packages
5. **Construction** — execution

### CMB process vs DBIA standard — comparison
| DBIA phase | CMB step | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Programming | Step 1 — Initial Consultation + Vision Planning | Aligned |
| Schematic Design | Step 2 — Schematic Design | Same name |
| Design Development | Step 3 — Design Development | Same name |
| Construction Documents | Step 4 — Project Development | CMB combines CD + bidding here |
| Construction | Step 5 — Construction (separate contract) | Aligned |

**The CMB labels are slightly less industry-standard than DBIA**. "Project Development" for Construction Documents is internal CMB terminology. If clients ever come from architectural backgrounds, they'll be confused.

**Recommendation:** keep CMB's labels in marketing copy, but add a one-line industry-standard equivalent in the contract: "Project Development (industry equivalent: Construction Documents + Subcontractor Bidding)."

## Design retainer pricing — industry benchmarks

### Typical residential ranges
Per [Buildxact](https://www.buildxact.com/us/blog/preconstruction-costs/), [Suiter Business Builders](https://suiterbusinessbuilders.com/how-to-charge-for-pre-construction-services/), [Nicholas Design Build](https://nicholasdesignbuild.com/801454-2/), and [Jordan Homes of NC](https://jordanhomesofnc.com/our-most-frequently-asked-questions/):

| Project type | Retainer % of est. construction | Dollar range |
|---|---|---|
| Small remodel | 1-2% | $1,500 - $5,000 |
| Major remodel / addition | 2-3% | $5,000 - $15,000 |
| Mid-range custom new build | 2-3% | $10,000 - $25,000 |
| High-end custom new build | 2.5-5% | $20,000 - $50,000 |
| Architect-led design retainer | 8-15% of construction | Architects charge higher; design-build is competitive advantage |

### Structure best practices
- **Non-refundable** — protects design team's time
- **Credited against final contract price** — softens psychological cost to client
- **Phased payments** — 30% on signing, 30% at Step 2 completion, 40% at Step 3 completion
- **Hourly rate disclosed** as backup for items beyond scope
- **NTE (Not-To-Exceed) cap** — clients at premium price points often want this for budgeting

### CMB current state
The Site Visit App contract:
- Hourly rates $150/$250 — appropriate for premium positioning
- Retainer = client-specified amount, applied to first invoices — aligns with industry
- Non-refundable language present — correct
- NO NTE cap — consider adding for clients who request it

## The 3D walkthrough — CMB's hidden weapon

Per CMB facts (from cmb-war-room/agents/cmb-facts.js):

> "CMB's most-valuable single process moment. Buyers regularly arrive with floor plans they think they want, walk through the 3D, and completely change the layout — kitchen on the other side of the living room, master moved, openings repositioned. Catching those changes before framing routinely prevents major change orders and lifelong regret."

This is genuinely uncommon among small design-build firms in mountain markets. Most clients don't see anything 3D until framing is up and it's too late to change.

### How to talk about it
- **Not:** "We do 3D renderings" (sounds like a feature)
- **Yes:** "Before we frame a single wall, you'll walk through your kitchen, sit at your island, see the morning light hit your bedroom — and tell us what to change. That's the moment most builders save for after framing, when changes cost real money. We move it forward, so changes cost a few hours of design time instead of a few thousand dollars."

### Apply to CMB Site Visit App
The Concept Images step (Step 3 in the new flow) already does this for individual rooms. The full 3D walkthrough happens in office mode during Step 2-3 of the Design-Build Program.

**Add a callout to the Sign-page contract:** "Before construction begins, you'll experience your home in full 3D — every room, every selection, every sightline. This is where most major layout decisions happen. Changes at this stage cost design time. Changes after framing cost real money."

## Premium positioning — how to charge more without arrogance

### What separates premium design-build from commodity

| Commodity DB | Premium DB |
|---|---|
| "We build homes" | "We bring clarity to your dreams" |
| Lowest bid wins | Right fit wins |
| Project = transaction | Project = relationship |
| Speak about cost | Speak about value |
| Client = customer | Client = neighbor |
| Process = box-tick | Process = considered art |
| Past work = list | Past work = story |

CMB's brand voice already lands in the premium column. The risk is process drift toward commodity under pressure.

### How premium builders charge premium prices

Per industry research and high-end-residential case studies:
- **Lead with their process, not their price** — clients buy the experience, not the line items
- **Show finished work, not specs** — photos of homes lived in by happy clients
- **Reference, don't list, past projects** — "the home we built off Armory Road" hits different than "1872 SF custom Mediterranean"
- **Decline some clients** — clients who price-shop won't fit; clients who want low-bid-with-CMB-quality won't either
- **The "fit" conversation** — explicit at sign: "we may not be the right builder for everyone, and that's okay"

### CMB's voice already does this
- "We may not be the cheapest" (already in voice rules)
- "If you've already got someone you trust, that's wonderful" (in approved examples)
- "Whether we end up being a fit" (in approved examples)

Maintain the discipline. The voice IS the premium positioning.

## Handling "I have plans from another architect"

The most common objection in design-build. Three ways to handle:

### Approach A: take the plans, build to them
**When:** client is already locked in, plans are good, scope is well-defined
**Pricing:** CMB charges construction-only contract; design retainer waived or reduced
**Risk:** CMB is in the role of GC, not design-build — sacrifices the unique value prop
**Recommendation:** acceptable for the right client, but make clear what CMB does and doesn't take responsibility for

### Approach B: review and refine
**When:** client has plans but they're unfinished, conceptual, or have problems
**Pricing:** "Design refinement" engagement at hourly, separate from build
**Risk:** client may resist paying for "fixing" their architect's work
**Recommendation:** frame as "we'd do a design review to identify any constructability or budget issues — typically 4-8 hours at $250/hr — and you decide what to do next"

### Approach C: politely decline
**When:** plans have fundamental problems (over-budget by 50%+, structurally questionable, energy code violations)
**Language:** "I'd love to help, and I want to be straight with you: the plans as drawn would push the build well above what you've mentioned for budget. Either we'd need to redesign significantly, or you'd be better served finding a builder who can work within whatever budget the plans really need."

### Apply to CMB Site Visit App
Add a project-type option: "Build to architect-provided plans (no design)" — triggers a different process flow that:
- Skips Steps 1-3 of the Design-Build Program
- Adjusts retainer accordingly
- Surfaces a contract addendum acknowledging limited design responsibility

## Contract review — CMB's current Design-Build Agreement vs industry standards

### Strengths to preserve
- Clear 5-step program with deliverables
- Hourly rate transparency ($150 designer / $250 principal-engineering-consultant)
- Non-refundable retainer applied to invoices
- First right of refusal on construction
- 1-year warranty disclosure (MT 28-2-2201 compliant)
- Sentence-case headings (not all-caps) ✓
- 200% design licensing fee for unauthorized use (strong deterrent)

### Gaps to address
1. **First right of refusal — no time window stated.** Industry standard: 30-60 days after Step 4 completion. Without a time window, the right could be argued to expire or be unenforceable.

2. **First right of refusal — no price-match mechanism.** Industry standard: client provides another builder's written bid; CMB has X days to match within X% range.

3. **200% design licensing fee — verify Montana enforceability.** Liquidated damages clauses must be reasonable, not punitive, to be enforceable. 200% may face challenge. Worth Montana construction attorney review.

4. **No "Not-To-Exceed" cap on hourly preconstruction fees.** Clients at premium price points often want a NTE. Consider offering as a 10-15% premium over hourly rates.

5. **Termination fee — minimum $5,000 or 10% of estimated construction.** The "estimated construction" reference is ambiguous when the estimate hasn't been finalized. Tighten language.

6. **Change-order language general, not specific.** MT 28-2-2201 requires payment schedule and change-order procedure disclosure. Add specific change-order markup percentage (e.g., "Change orders during Construction will carry our standard 35% markup over direct cost").

7. **Mediation/arbitration clause** — currently mentions mediation then Flathead County District Court. Industry-standard design-build contracts often add AAA Construction Industry Arbitration as a step. Consider — depends on whether CMB wants the speed of arbitration or the deterrent of court.

### Apply to CMB Site Visit App
Run the contract by a Montana construction attorney once. The 200% design fee, first-right-of-refusal mechanics, and change-order specificity are the highest-value targets for legal review.

## Sales process — when does owner-led shift to dedicated salesperson?

Per industry data:
- **Under $2M revenue:** Owner does all sales
- **$2-5M:** Owner does first conversation; PM or design lead handles design-development client meetings
- **$5-10M:** Dedicated "design consultant" or "client experience lead" runs sales; owner gets the close
- **$10M+:** Sales team with full pipeline management

### CMB current state
Owner-led (Matt) is appropriate. As CMB grows past ~$3M annual revenue, consider hiring a design-consultant role to handle first conversations, freeing Matt for closes and complex client management.

2. Two-story townhouse in South Kalispell
3. Custom home in Whitefish off Armory Road

### How to use them in sales conversation
- **Reference, don't list:** "Last year we did a home off Armory Road that had a similar layout challenge — happy to share what we learned."
- **Volunteer specifics ONLY when asked.** Don't lead with "look at our portfolio."

- **Match the reference to the prospect:** family residential → Flathead Lake home; LLC/commercial → West Glacier Building.

- When generating the SOW narrative, reference 1 past project that matches the prospect type
- Match logic: residential family → Flathead Lake / Armory Road; commercial/LLC → West Glacier; remodel/addition → East Kalispell; townhouse/multi-unit → South Kalispell
